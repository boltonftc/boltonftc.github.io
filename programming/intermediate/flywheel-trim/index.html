Flywheel Trim: Dialing In Your Shot

The Volume Knob Problem

You have a flywheel shooter that works. Press B, it spins up. Fire a ball, it launches. But here is the thing — you set the power to 1.0 (full blast) and that is the only option.

Imagine a speaker with no volume knob. It is either off or maximum. Want to have a quiet conversation? Too bad — full volume. Want to listen to music while studying? Full volume. That is what your shooter is right now: all or nothing.

In a real match, full power is not always what you want:

  • Close to the basket? Full power overshoots — the ball

hits the backboard and bounces out

  • Far from the basket? Full power might not be enough arc

— the ball flies flat and misses

  • Ball is lighter than expected? It launches too far
  • Battery is at 11V instead of 13V? Motor has less power

— your shots fall short

You need a way to adjust on the fly, without stopping the code and recompiling.

💡 Key Idea
**Real FTC teams tune their shooters mid-match.** A driver or

operator uses a stick or button to trim the flywheel power up or down. This is faster than guessing a hardcoded value and lets you adapt to changing conditions on the field.

What Is "Trim"?

Trim is a concept borrowed from aviation. When a pilot sets cruise altitude, they do not hold the control stick in one position the entire flight. They set a trim tab — a small adjustment that holds the plane's nose at the right angle without constant effort.

Your flywheel trim works the same way:

  • There is a base power (like cruise speed) — a reasonable

default that works for most shots

  • The right stick trims that power up or down — a

multiplier that stays where you put it

  • Let go of the stick, the trim stays — you do not have to

hold it

Think of it like this: 

actual power = base power × trim multiplier

| Trim | Effect | When to use | |------|--------|-------------| | 1.0× | Normal — base power | Most shots | | 0.7× | Softer — 70% of base | Close range, lighter balls | | 1.5× | Harder — 150% of base | Long range, low battery |

💡 Key Idea
**Trim is a multiplier, not an absolute value.** A trim of

1.0× means "use the base power as-is." Pushing the stick up slowly increases the multiplier. Pushing down decreases it. The multiplier is sticky — it stays where you leave it.

The Constants

Before we write the loop code, let's set up the numbers. You will declare these as variables at the top of your OpMode, before the waitForStart() line.

| Constant | Value | What It Means | |----------|-------|---------------| | FLYWHEEL_BASE_POWER | 0.65 | 65% motor power — a good default for mid-range shots | | flywheelTrim | 1.0 | Starting multiplier (no adjustment yet) | | TRIM_RATE | 0.4 | How fast the trim changes per second at full stick | | TRIM_DEADZONE | 0.15 | Ignore stick inputs smaller than 15% (prevents drift) | | TRIM_MIN | 0.5 | Lowest trim allowed (50% of base = very soft shot) | | TRIM_MAX | 2.0 | Highest trim allowed (200% of base = maximum power) |

Why 0.65 for the base? Because it gives you room to go both up (for long-range shots) and down (for close range). If the base were 1.0, you could only trim down. If it were 0.3, normal shots would be too weak.

💡 Key Idea
**Why a deadzone?** Joysticks are not perfect. Even when you

are not touching the stick, it might report 0.02 or -0.05 instead of exactly 0.0. The deadzone ignores these tiny ghost inputs so your trim does not slowly drift while your hands are on the driving sticks.

How the Loop Code Works

Here is the flow that runs every loop iteration:

  1. Is the flywheel on? Check if the motor power is above

0.01. If not, skip trim — there is nothing to trim.

  1. How much time passed? Calculate dtSec (delta time in

seconds) since the last loop. This makes the trim rate consistent regardless of how fast the loop runs.

  1. Read the stick. gamepad1.right_stick_y gives a value

from -1.0 (pushed up) to +1.0 (pushed down). We negate it so that pushing up increases the trim (feels natural).

  1. Apply the deadzone. If the stick is barely moved (less

than 15%), ignore it.

  1. Update the trim multiplier. Add `stickInput × TRIM_RATE

× dtSec to flywheelTrim. Clamp it between TRIM_MIN` and TRIM_MAX so it never goes to crazy values.

  1. Calculate final power. FLYWHEEL_BASE_POWER × flywheelTrim,

clamped to [0.0, 1.0].

  1. Apply it. shooterMotor.setPower(flywheelPower).
💡 Key Idea
**Delta time (`dtSec`)** makes the trim rate independent of

how fast the control loop runs. On a real robot, the loop might run 100 times per second when the CPU is free, or 50 times when it is busy reading sensors. By multiplying by dtSec — the actual seconds the last loop took — the trim changes at the same real-world speed no matter what. Push the stick up for one second and you get the same trim change whether the loop ran fast or slow. This is the standard approach used on real FTC robots and in professional robotics systems.

final double FLYWHEEL_BASE_POWER = 0.65;

final double TRIM_RATE = 0.4;

final double TRIM_MAX = 2.0;
long lastTrimTime = System.nanoTime();

Let's break it down:

  • FLYWHEEL_BASE_POWER — the "normal" power level. Marked

final because it should never change at runtime.

  • flywheelTrim — the multiplier that the stick adjusts.

Starts at 1.0 (no change). Not final — this one changes!

  • TRIM_RATE — how fast the multiplier moves per second at

full stick deflection (0.4 means it takes ~2.5 seconds to go from 1.0 to 2.0 at full stick).

  • TRIM_DEADZONE — the "ignore below this" threshold.
  • TRIM_MIN / TRIM_MAX — the multiplier limits.
  • lastTrimTime — timestamp for calculating dtSec.

System.nanoTime() gives nanoseconds (billionths of a second) — we will convert to seconds in the loop.

>> Type This Code
// Right stick Y trims flywheel power (sticky).


// Stick centered = keep current trim. Up = increase, down = decrease.
double flywheelPower = 0.0;
boolean flywheelOn = Math.abs(shooterMotor.getPower()) > 0.01;
if (flywheelOn) {
    long now = System.nanoTime();
    double dtSec = (now - lastTrimTime) / 1_000_000_000.0;
    lastTrimTime = now;
    double stickInput = -gamepad1.right_stick_y;
    if (Math.abs(stickInput) > TRIM_DEADZONE) {
        flywheelTrim += stickInput  TRIM_RATE  dtSec;
        flywheelTrim = Math.max(TRIM_MIN, Math.min(TRIM_MAX, flywheelTrim));
    }
    flywheelPower = FLYWHEEL_BASE_POWER * flywheelTrim;
    flywheelPower = Math.max(0.0, Math.min(1.0, flywheelPower));
} else {
    lastTrimTime = System.nanoTime();
}
shooterMotor.setPower(flywheelPower);




// Show flywheel status with trim info
telemetry.addData("Flywheel", flywheelOn
    ? String.format("ON (%.0f%% | trim %.1fx)", flywheelPower * 100, flywheelTrim)
    : "off");

Walk through the code line by line:

  1. flywheelPower = 0.0 — start at zero. If the flywheel

is off, it stays zero.

  1. flywheelOn — checks if the flywheel is currently

spinning (from the B-toggle you wrote earlier).

  1. If on: calculate time delta, read the stick, apply

deadzone, update the trim multiplier, compute final power.

  1. If off: just reset the timestamp so the first loop

after turning on does not get a huge dtSec spike.

  1. shooterMotor.setPower(flywheelPower) — applies the

trimmed power (or 0 if the flywheel is off).

  1. Telemetry — now shows power percentage AND the trim

multiplier, so you can see exactly what is happening.

Why This Matters on a Real Robot

On a real FTC robot, the flywheel trim is one of the most important driver controls. Here is why:

  • Battery voltage drops during a match. A freshly charged

13V battery delivers more power than a tired 11V battery. Trim up to compensate.

  • Balls vary. Foam balls from different manufacturers have

slightly different density and grip. One ball flies farther than another at the same power. Trim adjusts for this.

  • Position changes. When you are closer to the basket,

trim down. When you are farther, trim up. Good drivers learn the trim settings for each scoring position.

  • Warming up. Motor friction changes as the motor heats up

during a match. A cold flywheel may need more power than a warm one.

💡 Key Idea
**The best FTC shooters are not the most powerful — they are

the most consistent.** Trim gives the driver real-time control over consistency. A team with trim can adapt in seconds. A team without trim has to hope their hardcoded power works for every situation.

What You Learned

  • Trim is a sticky multiplier that adjusts a base power

level — the driver changes it with the right stick

  • Delta time (dtSec) makes the rate loop-speed

independent — a standard technique used on real FTC robots and in professional robotics systems

  • Deadzones prevent ghost stick inputs from causing drift
  • Clamping (min/max) keeps values in a safe range
  • System.nanoTime() provides high-resolution timestamps

for time-based calculations

  • This coding pattern — read input, apply rate × time, clamp —

appears everywhere: camera smoothing, motor ramping, PID controllers, animation systems
