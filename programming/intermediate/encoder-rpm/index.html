Encoder: Reading Flywheel RPM

How Fast Is It Spinning?

In the last lesson you wired up the flywheel shooter and got balls launching. You set the motor power to 1.0 and the wheel spun up. But how fast is it actually spinning? What RPM — revolutions per minute — does it reach? And how much does each shot slow it down?

💡 Key Idea
**RPM** stands for **Revolutions Per Minute** — the number of

full turns a shaft completes every 60 seconds. A 312 RPM motor makes 312 complete rotations each minute at full speed.

Right now you are flying blind — you set a power and hope for the best. To build a consistent shooter, you need feedback: a way to measure the actual wheel speed in real time.

💡 Key Idea
**Feedback** means measuring what is actually happening, not

just what you told the system to do. Without feedback, you are guessing. With feedback, you can measure, compare, and adjust.

What Is an Encoder?

An encoder is a sensor built into the motor that counts tiny electrical pulses as the output shaft turns. Each pulse represents a small fraction of a rotation.

Your goBILDA 312 RPM motor produces 537.7 counts per revolution (CPR). That means for every full turn of the output shaft, the encoder generates 537.7 electrical pulses. By counting how many pulses arrive each second, you can calculate exactly how fast the motor is spinning.

💡 Key Idea
An **encoder** counts pulses per rotation. Your motor has

537.7 CPR — that is 537.7 ticks for every full revolution of the output shaft.

From Ticks to RPM

The FTC SDK gives you the method: 

double ticksPerSec = shooterMotor.getVelocity();

This returns the encoder velocity in ticks per second. To convert to RPM:

  • Divide by 537.7 — converts ticks to revolutions
  • Multiply by 60 — converts per-second to per-minute
double rpm = (ticksPerSec / 537.7) * 60.0;

💡 Key Idea
**RPM from encoder:** `RPM = (ticks per sec / CPR) × 60`

For your goBILDA motor: RPM = (ticksPerSec / 537.7) * 60.0

Part 1 — Read the Encoder

The encoder is already wired — it is built into the motor you set up in the shooter lesson. You just need to call getVelocity() and convert the result.

Important: getVelocity() is only available on DcMotorEx, not the base DcMotor class. Your shooter motor is already in the hardware map as a DcMotorEx, so you just need to cast it when you read it.

Let's break that down:

  1. Cast the shooter motor to DcMotorEx to access getVelocity()
  2. getVelocity() returns ticks per second
  3. Divide by 537.7 CPR → revolutions per second
  4. Multiply by 60 → revolutions per minute
  5. Display the RPM on the Driver Hub telemetry

Part 3 — Watch the Whiteboard Graph

Look at the whiteboard on your pit station. You will see a live RPM graph — a scrolling 10-second window showing:

  • Green line — the actual flywheel RPM (from the encoder)
  • Gray dashed line — the target RPM (power × 312)

Spin Up

With the flywheel running, watch the green line curve upward. Notice it does not jump instantly to 312. It follows a smooth exponential curve — that is the flywheel's inertia. The heavy wheel takes time to accelerate.

Fire and Recover

While at full speed, fire a ball with the indexer. Watch the graph — the green line dips about 20%, then climbs back. That dip is why real teams wait for recovery between shots.

Compare

The RPM number on the Driver Hub telemetry and the green line on the whiteboard should match. The graph gives you the visual story; the telemetry gives you the exact number.

💜 Hint
**Pro tip:** On a real robot, you would use this RPM feedback

to decide when the flywheel is "ready to fire." A common threshold is 90% of target RPM — if the encoder shows the wheel is above that threshold, you allow the indexer to fire.

What You Learned

  • RPM = Revolutions Per Minute — how fast the shaft turns
  • Encoders count electrical pulses as the motor shaft turns
  • Your motor produces 537.7 ticks per revolution
  • getVelocity() returns ticks per second
  • Converting to RPM: divide by CPR, multiply by 60
  • The spin-up curve shows the flywheel's inertia in action
  • Each shot causes a measurable RPM dip

What's Next

You can now measure the flywheel speed, but you cannot control it precisely. What if you want the flywheel to hold exactly 250 RPM regardless of shots? That requires a PID controller -- a feedback loop that automatically adjusts motor power to maintain your target speed. That is a future lesson.
