AprilTag Detection

The Robot That Can See

In the last lesson, you gave your robot a sense of position with the OTOS. It can now track where it is by watching the ground — like a computer mouse on wheels. But you also discovered the OTOS's weakness: drift. Drive around for 30 seconds and the numbers slowly wander away from reality.

What if the robot could look up, see a landmark, and instantly know exactly where it is — like a sailor spotting a lighthouse?

That is exactly what AprilTags do. They are black-and-white coded markers placed around the FTC field. When the robot's camera sees one, it can calculate its own position on the field with no drift at all. Every detection is a fresh, independent measurement — the errors do not pile up.

In this lesson, you will connect a camera to your robot, point it at AprilTags, and read detection data in telemetry. You will not build a full navigation system yet — that comes in the next lesson. For now, you are giving the robot eyes.

💡 Key Idea
**AprilTags** are coded visual markers. When the camera sees

one, the robot can calculate its exact position on the field. Unlike the OTOS, AprilTag detections do not drift — each one is an independent measurement.

What Is an AprilTag?

An AprilTag is a printed black-and-white square pattern — like a simplified QR code. Each tag has a unique ID number encoded in its grid of black and white cells. The camera does not just see "a square" — it reads the specific binary pattern and knows exactly which tag it is looking at.

FTC uses the 36h11 tag family. The "36" means the data area is a 6×6 grid of cells (36 total). The "h11" means the minimum "Hamming distance" between any two valid codes is 11 — translation: even if part of the tag is obscured or blurry, the camera can still identify it correctly. It is remarkably robust.

Tags on the FTC Field

Every FTC field has AprilTags mounted on the walls at specific, published locations. For the INTO THE DEEP season, there are eight tags numbered 11 through 18:

| Tag ID | Wall | Position | | --- | --- | --- | | 11, 12 | North (audience side) | Left and right | | 13, 14 | East (red alliance) | Left and right | | 15, 16 | West (blue alliance) | Left and right | | 17, 18 | South (far side) | Left and right |

Because the exact position of each tag on the field is published by FIRST, and because each tag has a unique ID, the robot can do some math: "I can see Tag 13 at a certain angle and distance, and I know Tag 13 is at coordinates (X, Y) on the field, so I must be at coordinates (A, B)."

💡 Key Idea
Each AprilTag has a unique **ID number** encoded in its

pattern. Because the exact field position of each tag is published by FIRST, seeing a tag tells the robot exactly where it is on the field.

The Limelight 3A

What Is It?

The Limelight 3A is a smart camera built specifically for FTC and FRC robotics. It is not just a webcam — it has its own processor inside that does all the heavy image processing. Your robot's Control Hub does not have to waste any CPU time finding AprilTags; the Limelight does all the work and sends back clean, ready-to-use results.

Think of it like this: a regular webcam sends a stream of raw images and says "here, you figure it out." The Limelight sends a stream of results and says *"I found Tag 13, it is 36 inches away at 15 degrees to the right."*

The Hardware

The Limelight 3A is a compact aluminum camera module (roughly the size of a deck of cards). It has:

  • A wide-angle camera lens on the front
  • A built-in processor (Raspberry Pi Compute Module)
  • Status LEDs on the front
  • A USB-C port on the back for connection and power

Connecting to the Control Hub

Unlike the OTOS (which uses I2C), the Limelight connects via USB. This is because it transfers much more data — full images and detection results — which requires the higher bandwidth of a USB connection.

To install:

  1. Mount the Limelight on your robot, facing forward (or

wherever you want the camera to look)

  1. Plug a USB-C cable from the Limelight directly into

one of the USB ports on the REV Control Hub

  1. That is it — power and data both flow through the single

USB cable

The Limelight powers itself from the USB connection, so there are no extra wires to worry about. One cable does everything.

💡 Key Idea
The Limelight 3A connects to the Control Hub via a single

USB-C cable that carries both power and data. It has its own built-in processor that handles all image processing — your robot code just reads the results.

MegaTag vs. MegaTag2

The Limelight has two different ways to compute your robot's position from AprilTag detections. Understanding the difference is important.

MegaTag (Vision Only)

MegaTag uses only the camera image to figure out the robot's position and heading. It looks at the shape of the tag in the image — how it is tilted, rotated, and scaled — and works backward to calculate where the camera must be.

The problem: when the robot can only see one tag, the heading estimate is very noisy. A small change in the tag's appearance angle creates a large swing in estimated yaw. With two or more tags, MegaTag can triangulate and the heading becomes much more stable.

MegaTag2 (Vision + IMU)

MegaTag2 solves the heading problem by cheating — in a smart way. Instead of guessing the robot's heading from the camera image, it asks the robot: *"What does your IMU say your heading is?"*

You feed the robot's IMU heading to the Limelight each loop iteration. The Limelight then locks in that heading and only uses the camera to figure out X and Y position. This is much more accurate, especially when only one tag is visible.

The tradeoff? MegaTag2 is only as good as your IMU heading. If the gyroscope has drifted, MegaTag2 will compute the wrong position. In practice, IMU heading drift is small enough over a 30-second autonomous that MegaTag2 is almost always the better choice.

💡 Key Idea
**MegaTag** = vision only (noisy heading with one tag).

MegaTag2 = vision + IMU heading (more accurate, especially with one or two tags). Most teams use MegaTag2.

Tips and Tricks for the Limelight 3A

Teams who have used the Limelight in competition have learned some hard lessons. Here are the most important:

1. Mount It Rigid

Any vibration or wobble in the camera mount will blur the image and reduce detection range. Use metal brackets and lock-tighten all screws. The Limelight should feel rock-solid.

2. Aim Carefully

The Limelight has a wide field of view, but tags need to be within that cone to be detected. Point the camera toward the wall where you expect to see tags during autonomous. Many teams angle the camera slightly upward to catch wall-mounted tags at longer range.

3. Check the Pipeline

Limelight pipelines are numbered 0–9. Each can be configured for a different task. Pipeline 0 is typically set up for AprilTag detection. Make sure you call pipelineSwitch(0) during initialization.

4. Watch the Distance

AprilTag detection works best within about 6 feet (72 inches). Beyond that, the tags become too small in the image for reliable detection. At close range (under 12 inches), the tag may fill the entire frame and detection can also fail. The sweet spot is 1 to 5 feet.

5. Lighting Matters

Bright, even lighting helps. Shadows across a tag can make one half appear darker than the other, confusing the detector. At competitions, venue lighting varies — practice under different conditions.

6. Feed the IMU Every Loop

For MegaTag2, you must call updateRobotOrientation() with your current IMU heading every loop iteration — not just once. The Limelight needs current heading data each time it processes a frame.

💡 Key Idea
**Key tips:** Mount rigid, aim toward tags, use pipeline 0,

best range is 1–5 feet, call updateRobotOrientation() every loop for MegaTag2.

The Limelight API

In the simulator, the Limelight is already installed on your virtual robot and mapped as "limelight" in the hardware map.

Here are the key classes and methods:

| Class / Method | What It Does | | --- | --- | | Limelight3A | The camera object from the hardware map | | .start() | Starts the processing pipeline | | .pipelineSwitch(0) | Selects pipeline 0 (AprilTag detection) | | .updateRobotOrientation(yaw) | Feeds IMU heading (degrees) for MegaTag2 | | .getLatestResult() | Returns an LLResult snapshot | | LLResult.isValid() | true if any tags were detected | | .getFiducialResults() | List of individual tag detections | | .getBotpose_MT2() | Robot field position from MegaTag2 | | FiducialResult.getFiducialId() | The tag's ID number | | BotPose.getX() / .getY() | Robot position on field (inches) | | BotPose.getYaw() | Robot heading (degrees) | | BotPose.getTagCount() | How many tags were used | | BotPose.getAvgDist() | Average distance to detected tags |

>> Type This Code
// Feed the OTOS heading to Limelight for MegaTag2


SparkFunOTOS.Pose2D otosForLL = otos.getPosition();
limelight.updateRobotOrientation(otosForLL.h);




// Get the latest detection result
LLResult llResult = limelight.getLatestResult();




if (llResult != null && llResult.isValid()) {
    // Tags detected! Show each one
    telemetry.addData("Tags", "%d detected", llResult.getFiducialResults().size());




    for (LLResultTypes.FiducialResult tag : llResult.getFiducialResults()) {
        telemetry.addData("  Tag ID", tag.getFiducialId());
        telemetry.addData("  Bearing", "%.1f°", tag.getTargetXDegrees());
    }




    // Show MegaTag2 field position (if available)
    LLResultTypes.BotPose mt2 = llResult.getBotpose_MT2();
    if (mt2 != null) {
        telemetry.addData("MT2 X", "%.1f in", mt2.getX());
        telemetry.addData("MT2 Y", "%.1f in", mt2.getY());
        telemetry.addData("MT2 Yaw", "%.1f°", mt2.getYaw());
        telemetry.addData("MT2 Tags", mt2.getTagCount());
        telemetry.addData("MT2 Dist", "%.1f in", mt2.getAvgDist());
    }
} else {
    telemetry.addData("Tags", "No tags detected");
}

Let's trace through what this code does:

  1. Feed heading — The OTOS gives us the robot's current

heading. We pass it to the Limelight so MegaTag2 can use it for more accurate localization.

  1. Get result — getLatestResult() returns the camera's

most recent frame analysis. It might be null (camera not ready) or invalid (no tags visible).

  1. Check validity — isValid() tells us whether any tags

were actually detected in this frame.

  1. Show individual tags — Each FiducialResult has the tag's

ID number and its bearing (left/right angle from camera center).

  1. Show MegaTag2 — The robot's estimated field position

based on the detected tags and the IMU heading. This is the "big picture" answer: where am I on the field?

  • AprilTags are coded visual markers (36h11 family) placed

on FTC field walls at published locations

  • The Limelight 3A is a smart camera with its own processor

— it handles all image processing internally

  • It connects via USB-C to the Control Hub (one cable for

power and data)

  • MegaTag uses vision only (noisy heading with one tag);

MegaTag2 uses vision + IMU heading (much more accurate)

  • You must call updateRobotOrientation() every loop

  • Detection range is best within 1 to 5 feet (12–60

  • Tag detections give absolute field position with zero

  • The OTOS tracks continuously but drifts; AprilTags

give perfect snapshots but only intermittently
