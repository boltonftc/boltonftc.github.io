SparkFun OTOS

Where Am I?

You have been driving the robot around by hand — pushing sticks, watching it move, and guiding it yourself. That works great for TeleOp. But what about Autonomous?

In Autonomous, there is no driver. The robot has 30 seconds to score points entirely on its own. No joystick. No human. Just code. And the very first problem your code has to solve is deceptively simple:

Where am I on the field?

Think about it. You can tell the robot to drive forward 24 inches. But how does the robot know when it has actually gone 24 inches? You can tell it to turn right 90 degrees. But how does it know when it has actually turned 90 degrees? Without some way to measure its own movement, the robot is driving blind.

This lesson introduces a sensor that solves exactly this problem — the SparkFun OTOS.

💡 Key Idea
In Autonomous, the robot must know **where it is** and

how far it has moved without any human input. Position-tracking sensors like the OTOS make this possible.

What Is OTOS?

OTOS stands for Optical Tracking Odometry Sensor. That is a mouthful, so let's break it down one word at a time:

  • Optical — it uses light (specifically a tiny camera) to

see the surface below it

  • Tracking — it follows its own movement over time
  • Odometry — the measurement of distance traveled

(from the Greek hodos meaning "path" and metron meaning "measure")

  • Sensor — a device that detects physical input and

reports it to the robot's brain

Put it all together: the OTOS is a sensor that uses a tiny camera to measure how far the robot has traveled by watching the ground beneath it.

💡 Key Idea
**OTOS** = **O**ptical **T**racking **O**dometry **S**ensor.

It uses a downward-facing camera to measure how far the robot has moved by watching the ground surface.

It is made by SparkFun Electronics, a company based in Colorado that builds sensors and electronics boards for makers, students, and robotics teams. The full product name is the "SparkFun Qwiic Optical Tracking Odometry Sensor" but everyone in FTC just calls it the OTOS.

How It Works — The Computer Mouse on Your Robot

You already know a device that works almost exactly the same way as the OTOS. It is sitting next to your keyboard right now.

A computer mouse.

Look at the bottom of a modern optical mouse and you will see a small light (usually red or infrared) and a tiny lens. That light illuminates the surface under the mouse, and a miniature camera inside takes thousands of pictures per second of the surface texture — the tiny scratches, bumps, and patterns in your desk or mousepad.

A chip inside the mouse compares each frame to the one before it. If the pattern shifted two pixels to the left, the mouse knows you moved it to the left. If it shifted three pixels up, you moved it forward. By adding up all those tiny shifts, frame by frame, the mouse tracks your hand's position with incredible precision.

The OTOS does the same thing, but for your robot.

It points a tiny camera straight down at the game mat. The foam tiles have a slightly textured surface — just like a mousepad has a texture. The OTOS takes thousands of images per second of that texture and compares them frame by frame. Every tiny shift tells the sensor how far the robot moved and in which direction.

💡 Key Idea
The OTOS works just like an **optical mouse** — sensor facing

down, tracking the surface beneath it. It photographs the ground thousands of times per second and tracks tiny shifts between frames to measure movement. It reports X position, Y position, and heading.

But the OTOS has a superpower that a regular mouse does not: a built-in IMU (Inertial Measurement Unit — the same type of gyroscope sensor you used in the IMU Heading lesson). This gives it an independent measurement of rotation. The optical tracking handles X and Y movement, and the IMU handles heading. Together, they give you a complete picture: *"I am at position (X, Y) and facing heading H."*

What It Reports

Every time you ask the OTOS for data, it gives you three numbers packed into a structure called a Pose2D:

| Value | What It Means | | --- | --- | | x | Left/right position (inches from where you started) | | y | Forward/backward position (inches from where you started) | | h | Heading — which direction the robot is facing (degrees) |

It can also report velocity (how fast you are moving right now) and acceleration (how quickly your speed is changing), but position is the most important one for autonomous navigation.

The Hardware

What It Looks Like

The SparkFun OTOS is a small green circuit board, roughly the size of a postage stamp (about 1 inch by 1 inch). On the bottom is the optical sensor lens — a tiny dark window that faces the ground. On the top are the electronic components and a connector port.

The Wires — I2C Connection

The OTOS connects to the robot using an I2C bus (pronounced "I-squared-C" or "I-two-C"). I2C is a standard communication protocol that lets multiple sensors share just two data wires. The connection uses four wires:

| Wire | Color | Purpose | | --- | --- | --- | | VCC | Red | Power (3.3V from the hub) | | GND | Black | Ground (completes the circuit) | | SDA | Blue | Data line (sensor sends readings here) | | SCL | Yellow | Clock line (timing signal so both sides stay in sync) |

The OTOS uses a Qwiic connector — a small, keyed plug that only fits one way. You cannot plug it in backwards. This is a huge advantage over bare-wire connections where you might accidentally swap wires and fry the sensor.

Connecting to the Control Hub

On the REV Control Hub or Expansion Hub, you will find I2C ports labeled 0 through 3. Each port has a matching Qwiic- compatible connector. To install the OTOS:

  1. Plug one end of a Qwiic cable into the OTOS board
  2. Plug the other end into any I2C port on the Control Hub
  3. Mount the OTOS on the underside of the robot chassis,

pointing straight down at the ground

  1. Make sure the sensor is centered on the robot (or as close

to center as your design allows)

That is it — no soldering, no stripping wires, no figuring out which pin is which. The Qwiic system makes it plug-and-play.

💡 Key Idea
The OTOS connects via **I2C** using a **Qwiic cable** — a

keyed four-wire connector (power, ground, data, clock) that plugs directly into the Control Hub. No soldering required.

The Critical Detail — Height Off the Mat

Here is the one thing that trips up almost every team the first time they install an OTOS:

The distance between the sensor lens and the ground matters. A lot.

The optical sensor has a tiny lens with a very specific focal range. If the sensor is too close to the ground, the lens cannot focus and the image is a blur — like holding a book one inch from your eyes. If the sensor is too far away, it sees too large an area and the individual surface details become too small to track reliably.

The sweet spot is approximately 10 millimeters — about the height of a pencil lying on its side, or roughly the thickness of your pinky finger. SparkFun's documentation recommends 10.0 mm from the bottom of the sensor to the playing surface.

What Happens at the Wrong Height

| Height | What Goes Wrong | | --- | --- | | Too low (< 8 mm) | Lens cannot focus. Tracking fails or reports wildly wrong values. Status flag warnOpticalTracking fires. | | Sweet spot (~10 mm) | Clear image. Accurate tracking. This is where you want to be. | | Too high (> 14 mm) | Image is too zoomed out. Surface details are too small. Tracking becomes noisy and unreliable. |

How Teams Get the Height Right

Most teams 3D-print a custom standoff or mounting bracket that holds the OTOS at exactly the right height. Some use stacked washers, nylon spacers, or carefully measured L-brackets. The key is that the mounting must be rigid — if the sensor bounces or vibrates during driving, the readings will be garbage.

💡 Key Idea
The OTOS lens must be approximately **10 mm** above the game

mat surface. Too close and it cannot focus. Too far and it loses detail. Most teams 3D-print a rigid mount to hold it at exactly the right height.

💜 Hint
**Pro tip:** After mounting, power on the robot and check the

OTOS status flags in telemetry. If warnOpticalTracking is true, the sensor cannot see the surface properly — adjust the height. You will learn how to check this in the coding section below.

Why Use OTOS at All?

You might be wondering: "We already have the IMU for heading and motor encoders for distance. Why add another sensor?"

Great question. Here is the problem with those other methods:

Motor Encoders Alone

Motor encoders count how many times the motor shaft has rotated. From that, you can calculate how far the wheel has turned. But — what if the wheel slips? On a mecanum robot, the wheels slip constantly during strafing movements. The encoder says the motor turned 1000 ticks, but the robot only moved half the expected distance because the rollers were sliding. Encoders measure what the motor did, not what the robot did.

IMU Alone

The IMU gives you heading (rotation), but it says nothing about X/Y position. You know which way the robot is facing, but not where it is on the field. And IMU heading drifts slowly over time — after a few minutes, it might be off by a few degrees.

OTOS Advantage

The OTOS tracks what the robot actually does, not what the motors commanded. If the wheels slip, the OTOS still sees the ground moving at the correct speed. It gives you X, Y, and heading — all in one sensor, all by watching the real motion.

The tradeoff? The OTOS also drifts over time. Every frame-to- frame comparison has a tiny measurement error, and those errors add up — just like a computer mouse would slowly lose its position if you dragged it around for two minutes straight on a bumpy desk. After driving around the field for 30 seconds of autonomous, the OTOS might be off by an inch or two.

That drift is manageable for short autonomous routines. In a later lesson, you will learn how to correct for it using a completely different kind of sensor. But for now, the OTOS gives you something no other single sensor can: a complete position estimate (X, Y, heading) from one device.

💡 Key Idea
The OTOS measures what the **robot** actually does, not what

the motors commanded. It gives you X, Y, and heading in one sensor. The tradeoff is that it drifts over time — each reading has a small error that accumulates.

The OTOS in Our Simulator

In the real world, you would wire a physical SparkFun OTOS board to your Control Hub. In our simulator, the OTOS is already "installed" on the virtual robot and ready to use.

The simulated OTOS behaves realistically:

  • It tracks your robot's position as you drive around
  • It accumulates small errors over time (drift), just like

the real sensor

  • The built-in IMU provides heading data
  • All the same API methods work identically

You access it from the hardware map using the name "sensor_otos": 

SparkFunOTOS otos = hardwareMap.get(SparkFunOTOS.class, "sensor_otos");

This gives you an OTOS object with methods to configure the sensor, read position, reset tracking, and more.

Key API Methods

Here are the methods you will use most often:

| Method | What It Does | | --- | --- | | setLinearUnit(DistanceUnit.INCH) | Sets position units to inches | | setAngularUnit(AngleUnit.DEGREES) | Sets heading units to degrees | | calibrateImu() | Calibrates the built-in gyroscope (keep robot still!) | | resetTracking() | Resets X, Y, and heading to zero | | getPosition() | Returns a Pose2D with current x, y, and h | | getVelocity() | Returns a Pose2D with current velocity | | setPosition(pose) | Manually sets the current position |

The Pose2D object has three fields: .x, .y, and .h. When you call getPosition(), you get back a Pose2D and can read each value separately: 

SparkFunOTOS.Pose2D pos = otos.getPosition();


double xPos = pos.x;      // left/right offset in inches
double yPos = pos.y;      // forward/backward offset in inches
double heading = pos.h;    // heading in degrees

✅ To Do
Click the link below to open your code at the OTOS setup

[ MecanumDrive.java › OTOS : Setup ]
✅ To Do
Add this code in the OTOS Setup section (right below the

>> Type This Code
// Get the OTOS from the hardware map


SparkFunOTOS otos = hardwareMap.get(SparkFunOTOS.class, "sensor_otos");




// Configure units: inches for distance, degrees for heading
otos.setLinearUnit(DistanceUnit.INCH);
otos.setAngularUnit(AngleUnit.DEGREES);




// Calibrate the built-in IMU (robot must be still!)
otos.calibrateImu();




// Reset position to (0, 0, 0)
otos.resetTracking();




telemetry.addData("OTOS", "Initialized!");
telemetry.update();

💜 Hint
**Important:** The robot must be **completely still** when

calibrateImu() runs. The IMU calibration measures the "zero point" for rotation. If the robot is moving during calibration, the heading will be wrong for the entire match.

Test Your OTOS!

✅ To Do
**Test 1 — Forward:** Drive the robot straight forward about

two feet. Watch the OTOS Y value increase. It should read somewhere around 24 inches (give or take — the OTOS is not perfect!). Then drive backward to the start and watch it return toward zero.

✅ To Do
**Test 2 — Strafe:** Start from rest and strafe the robot to

the right about one foot. Watch OTOS X increase to around 12 inches. The Y value should stay close to zero (you did not move forward or backward).

✅ To Do
**Test 3 — Rotation:** Spin the robot 90 degrees clockwise.

Watch OTOS Heading change. It should read approximately -90 degrees (clockwise is negative in the OTOS coordinate system). Spin back and it should return toward zero.

✅ To Do
**Test 4 — Reset:** Drive the robot to a random spot on the

field. Press the right bumper on the gamepad. All three values (X, Y, Heading) should snap back to zero — and the IMU heading resets too, since both resets are on the same button. Now drive forward — the OTOS measures from this new starting point.

✅ To Do
**Test 5 — Drift:** This is the important one. Drive the robot

in a big square on the field — forward, right, backward, left — and end up back where you started. Look at the OTOS values. Are they exactly zero? Probably not. The small errors from each movement have added up. This is drift — and it is the main limitation of any dead-reckoning sensor.

💡 Key Idea
**Drift** is the accumulation of small measurement errors over

time. The OTOS is very accurate for short movements, but after driving around for a while, the reported position slowly diverges from reality. This is normal — all dead-reckoning sensors have this property.

What You Learned

  • OTOS stands for Optical Tracking Odometry Sensor — it

uses a tiny camera to track movement across the ground surface, just like a computer mouse

  • The sensor connects via I2C using a Qwiic cable — four

wires (power, ground, data, clock) in a keyed connector

  • The lens must be approximately 10 mm above the playing

surface — too close or too far causes tracking failures

  • The OTOS reports Pose2D: x position, y position, and

heading — a complete position estimate from one sensor

  • It has a built-in IMU for heading and optical tracking

for X/Y movement

  • Unlike motor encoders, the OTOS measures what the robot

actually does, not what the motors commanded — so wheel slip does not fool it

  • The OTOS drifts over time because tiny frame-to-frame

errors accumulate — this is the fundamental tradeoff of dead-reckoning sensors
