FTC Speed-Run Schedule

Box of Parts → Kids Driving a Mecanum Robot
Spring 2026 Exploratory Program — Building Excitement for Fall Competition Season
Version 0.2 — February 25, 2026

Team: 5–8 students, Grades 7–8 Meetings: 6× Wed (2 hr) + Sun buffers Target: First drive at Meeting 5 Code depth: Minimal — deploy & drive

Program Goals & Student Outcomes

This is a spring exploratory program — not competition season. The purpose is to give students a positive, hands-on introduction to FIRST Tech Challenge so they enter the fall season with confidence, context, and momentum. The emphasis is on experience and excitement, not depth.

What Students Will Do

  • Learn what FIRST Tech Challenge is and how a season works
  • Assemble a competition-legal mecanum drivebase from a kit
  • Wire motors and electronics to a REV Control Hub
  • See what Java code looks like and understand what it does at a high level
  • Compile and deploy code to a robot using Android Studio
  • Drive a mecanum robot with holonomic movement (strafe, rotate, translate)
  • Work in pairs, collaborate, problem-solve as a team
  • Walk away excited to do more in the fall

What This Program Is Not

  • Not a Java programming course
  • Not competition prep
  • Not a deep dive into CAD, 3D printing, or mechanical design
  • Not a substitute for fall season ramp-up
  • Not high-pressure

Mentor Prep vs. Student Work — Division of Responsibility

Proposed mentor/student work balance, chosen to maximize what the students do hands-on while maintaining a high probability of success within the condensed 6-meeting timeline.

What the Mentor Does (Behind the Scenes)

  • Inventory all parts before Meeting 1
  • Pre-sort hardware into labeled bags by assembly step
  • Create simplified build guides and printed cheat sheets
  • Install & configure Android Studio on all laptops
  • Clone the FTC SDK, verify it builds, pre-load the TeleOp OpMode
  • Pre-wire the robot independently to verify cable routing, motor port mapping, and power-on, photograph everything, then disconnect
  • Update Control Hub and Driver Hub firmware
  • Pre-configure the hardware map
  • Charge batteries before every meeting
  • Troubleshoot any issues between meetings
  • Have fallback plans for code deploy

What the Mentor Does NOT Do

  • Assemble any part of the robot chassis
  • Mount the motors or wheels
  • Wire the motors or battery during meetings
  • Mount the Control Hub to the chassis
  • Press the Build or Deploy buttons
  • Drive the robot first (except a brief safety check)
  • Fix problems for students
  • Lecture for long stretches

Logistics

  • Wednesday meetings are held at the school, after school, 2 hours each
  • Mentor transports the robot, tools, and supplies to and from the school each Wednesday via rolling cart
  • Robot and all materials stay with the mentor between meetings for prep work, troubleshooting, and battery charging
  • Optional Sunday buffer meetings are held at the FRC team facility, not at the school
  • A second FTC team meets at the FRC facility — Sunday sessions are joint meetings so students from both FTC teams can learn from each other and build connections
  • No special facility requirements at the school — a classroom table and open floor space for driving is sufficient. No power tools, no soldering, no metal cutting.

Everything needed for assembly and operation. All items are provided by the mentor — nothing required from the school beyond table space and floor space.

Meeting 1 — Unbox & Start Build

Wed Apr 29

Kickoff 20 min

  • Welcome, team intros, ground rules
  • "What is FTC?" — show a 3-minute highlight reel video
  • Show the goal: "In 5 meetings, you will be driving this robot"
  • Show the parts still in boxes

Inventory & Sort 20 min

  • Open the Strafer kit — lay out every bag, identify components
  • Match parts to the build guide (printed copies for each pair)
  • Assign parts to labeled bins or areas on the table

Start Chassis Frame 70 min

  • Split into pairs, each pair owns one corner/side of the frame
  • Assemble U-channel rails, cross-members, motor mounts
  • Focus: proper bolt torque (snug, not gorilla-tight), correct orientation

Wrap-Up 10 min

  • Photo of progress. Clean up workspace.
  • Preview: "Next week we finish the chassis and it rolls"
Goal: Frame partially assembled, all parts identified
Your Prep (Before Meeting 1):
  • Inventory all parts, confirm nothing is missing from kits
  • Print simplified build guides (condensed from goBILDA docs) — one per pair
  • Pre-sort hardware into labeled bags if possible (M4×8, M4×12, etc.)
  • Set up all laptops with Android Studio + FtcRobotController project (verified building)
  • Write step-by-step "Deploy Code" cheat sheet for Meeting 3–4
  • Pre-wire the robot yourself to confirm motor ports, cable routing — take photos — then disconnect
  • Charge both batteries

Buffer — Finish Chassis (if needed)

Sun May 4 (optional)
  • Only use if chassis frame is not complete after Meeting 1
  • Continue frame assembly, mount motors, start wheel attachment
  • If not needed, skip — give the kids a break

Meeting 2 — Finish Chassis & Wheels

Wed May 6

Complete Frame Assembly 40 min

  • Finish any remaining frame bolting
  • Mount all 4 motors into motor mounts
  • Verify motor orientation (shaft side facing out toward wheels)

Attach Mecanum Wheels 40 min

  • Mount all 4 mecanum wheels onto motor shafts
  • Critical: Correct wheel orientation! Top rollers form an "X" pattern when viewed from above. Get this wrong and strafing breaks.
  • Tighten set screws. Spin each wheel by hand — should rotate freely.

Rolling Chassis Celebration 10 min

  • Flip it over. Push it around the room. It rolls!
  • This is a real milestone — take a team photo

Intro to Control Electronics 20 min

  • Show the REV Control Hub, battery, cables — don't install yet
  • Explain: "This is the brain. Next meeting we wire it up."
  • Show your pre-wiring photos: "Here's where everything plugs in"

Wrap-Up 10 min

  • Clean up. Preview next meeting.
Goal: Rolling chassis — 4 wheels, 4 motors, complete frame

Meeting 3 — Electronics & Wiring

Wed May 13

Mount Control Hub 20 min

  • Attach Control Hub to chassis using Dual Lock or standoffs
  • Position for easy access to ports and power switch

Wire Motors 40 min

  • Connect all 4 motor cables from motors to Control Hub motor ports
  • Use your pre-wiring photos as the guide
  • Label each cable (FL, FR, BL, BR) — this must match the hardware map in code
  • Route cables cleanly along the frame. Zip-tie to prevent snagging.

Battery & Power-On 20 min

  • Mount battery with Velcro/Dual Lock
  • Connect XT30 power cable from battery to Control Hub
  • Power on. Green LEDs on Control Hub = success.
  • Connect a laptop to Control Hub WiFi — see the dashboard

Intro to the Code 30 min

  • Everyone on laptops. Open the FtcRobotController project in Android Studio.
  • Show them the one file they care about: the TeleOp OpMode
  • "This is Java. These lines read the gamepad. These lines set motor power."
  • Show the Build button (green hammer). Show the Deploy button (green play).
  • Do NOT deploy yet — just orientation

Wrap-Up 10 min

  • Robot is wired and powered. Code is on laptops. Next: connect the two.
Goal: Robot powered on, green LEDs, all motors wired
Risk: Wiring usually takes longer than expected — Sunday buffer exists
Your Prep (Before Meeting 3):
  • Update Control Hub firmware using REV Hardware Client
  • Update Driver Hub firmware
  • Pre-configure the hardware map on the Control Hub (motor names: frontLeft, frontRight, backLeft, backRight)
  • Have the TeleOp OpMode ready in the project on all laptops
  • Test WiFi connection from a laptop to Control Hub

Buffer — Wiring Debug & Code Deploy Prep

Sun May 18 (recommended)
  • Plan on using this one. Wiring + first deploy is where things bog down.
  • Finish any incomplete wiring from Meeting 3
  • Practice the deploy workflow with 1–2 kids: Build → Connect → Deploy → Run OpMode
  • Bench-test: spin each motor from the Driver Station to verify correct port mapping
  • Confirm motor directions — some may need to be reversed in code or hardware map
  • This de-risks Meeting 4 so it goes smoothly

Meeting 4 — Code Deploy & Bench Test

Wed May 20

Deploy Code to Robot 40 min

  • Walk everyone through: Connect laptop to Control Hub WiFi → Build → Deploy
  • Each pair deploys from their laptop (or watch and follow along)
  • Select the TeleOp OpMode on the Driver Hub
  • Connect gamepads to Driver Hub

Bench Test 30 min

  • Robot on blocks / lifted off table — wheels spin freely
  • Press each gamepad stick direction, verify correct wheel response:
  • Left stick forward = all 4 wheels forward
  • Left stick sideways = mecanum strafe pattern (diagonal pairs)
  • Right stick left/right = tank turn
  • If a motor spins wrong direction → fix in code (motor.setDirection) or hardware map

Cable Management & Cleanup 20 min

  • Final zip-tie pass on all cables
  • Secure battery mount
  • Verify nothing dangles near wheels

Quick Floor Test 20 min

  • If bench test passes: set robot on floor, careful slow drive in a small area
  • Mentor drives first to verify behavior, then let 1–2 kids try
  • If something is wrong: note it, don't panic, fix before Meeting 5

Wrap-Up 10 min

  • "Next meeting: everyone drives. Bring your game face."
Goal: Code deployed, motors verified, robot moves on command
Your Prep (Before Meeting 4):
  • If Sunday buffer was used: verify everything still works, batteries charged
  • Have a backup plan: your laptop can deploy code if student laptops have issues
  • Print the "Deploy Cheat Sheet" for each pair
  • Prepare a "motor direction fix" cheat sheet (which line to change, what values)
  • Charge both batteries

Meeting 5 — FIRST DRIVE 🏁

Wed May 27

Pre-Flight Check 15 min

  • Power on, connect, select OpMode, verify gamepad
  • Quick bench test — still works? Go.

Everyone Drives 60 min

  • Set up a large open area (gym, hallway, cafeteria)
  • Each student gets 5–8 minutes of drive time
  • Start simple: forward, backward, turn
  • Then: strafe left, strafe right — this is the "whoa" moment for mecanum
  • Then: diagonal movement, combined translate + rotate
  • Non-driving students watch, coach, and swap in

Mini Challenge 30 min

  • Set up a simple obstacle course with cones, boxes, tape lines
  • Timed runs — who can navigate it fastest?
  • Emphasizes precision driving and mecanum advantages

Debrief 15 min

  • What surprised you? What was hard? What do you want to add?
  • Preview: "You built this. Next: what can it DO?"
  • Team photo with the robot they built and drove
Goal: Every student drives the robot. This is the payoff.

Meeting 6 — Obstacle Course Competition & What's Next

Wed Jun 3

Warm-Up Driving 15 min

  • Free drive time — shake off the rust, practice strafing and rotating

Obstacle Course Competition 50 min

  • Set up an obstacle course using cones, boxes, tape lines, and narrow gates
  • Course should require forward driving, strafing, precision turns, and parking in a target zone
  • Timed individual runs — each student gets 2–3 attempts
  • Track best times on a whiteboard or projected screen
  • Final bracket round with the top times
  • Award bragging rights (and maybe snacks) to the winner

What's Next: Season Preview 30 min

  • FTC game reveal happens ~September — that tells us what the robot needs to do
  • Show examples: arms, intakes, lifts, launchers from past seasons
  • Intro to sensors: distance, color, touch — the IMU they already have
  • Intro to autonomous: "The robot drives itself for 30 seconds"
  • Discuss summer plans: watch past game reveals, learn more Java, design mechanisms

Wrap-Up 10 min

  • Team discussion: who wants to focus on what? (driving, building, coding)
  • Set expectations for the season: registration, schedule, competitions
  • Celebrate — you went from a box of parts to a driving robot in 6 meetings
Goal: Fun competition, confident drivers, excited for the season
